> [!summary]
> Branching visual technique to explore topics, reveal structure, and surface connections.

> [!When to use]
> When a topic feels tangled, mind mapping helps you see structure fast; this note shows how to map and turn nodes into atomic notes, even if you are not artistic.

### When mind maps are the right tool
- you are exploring a new topic and do not know the structure yet,
- you need to brainstorm options before choosing a path,
- you want to surface relationships between ideas quickly.

### How to build a mind map step by step
- Starting with topics instead of questions leads to vague maps. Fix: always phrase the center as a question.
- Ex: "How do I make retrieval effortless?"
- Ex: capture, organize, link, retrieve, review, output.

### Converting mind map nodes into atomic notes
- Add context and an example.
- Take one important node and write it as a standalone note title.
- Add 1-3 links to neighboring nodes as related notes.

### Tools and formats that work well
- Paper and pen (fastest).
- Whiteboard or tablet.
- Obsidian canvas or Excalidraw-style diagrams.

### Pitfalls like messy maps with no next step
- Fix: limit main branches to 4-7 and prune.
- Fix: always convert at least 2 nodes into notes immediately.
- Fix: focus on questions, constraints, and examples, not aesthetics.

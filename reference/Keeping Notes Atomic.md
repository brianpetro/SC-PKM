> [!summary]
> Principle: one idea per note, enough context to stand alone, optimized for linking.

> [!When to use]
> If one note contains five ideas and none of them link well, this note shows the atomic rule so your notes become reusable, without losing context.
> If your notes turn into big blobs you cannot reuse, this gives you a simple way to split and link atomic notes, without falling into over-splitting.

### Definition and criteria for atomic notes
- Scope test: there is one primary claim, concept, or method.
- Standalone test: you can understand it without rereading the entire book/article/meeting transcript.
- Link test: it can link cleanly to related concepts without becoming a dumping ground.
- Singular in scope (one idea).
- Written for future retrieval (standalone context).
- Designed for linking (clear nouns, claims, and relationships).

### How to ensure standalone context
- A concrete example (your own, if possible).
- A one-sentence definition or claim.
- The boundary: Covers / Does not cover.

### When to split vs keep together
- The note answers more than one question.
- You find yourself wanting to link to "part of the note".
- The note contains multiple claims that could be true independently.

### A repeatable capture-to-atomic workflow
- Add one concrete example from your life/work.
- Run [[Atomic Notes Checklist]] quickly and refactor if needed.
- Put raw text in your inbox.

### Examples of good and bad atomic notes
- "Decision logs prevent re-deciding the same issue."
- "A weekly review resets projects and reduces anxiety."
- "Thoughts on productivity" (multiple ideas, unclear boundary)

### Linking practices that benefit from atomicity
- Prefer linking to [[Permanent Notes]] rather than linking to raw inbox captures.
- Add one relationship sentence around a link (why it is linked).
- Use links to express meaning and navigation, as described in [[Linking and Connecting Notes]].
- Link to "neighbors" (supports, contrasts, example of, prerequisite for).
- Link to a definition or principle you rely on (not just the topic).
- Add a short relationship sentence, not just a naked link.

### Audit and refactor process for non-atomic notes
- It has 3+ unrelated headings.
- It mixes source quotes, personal opinion, tasks, and decisions without separation.
- You avoid linking to it because it feels "too big" or "too messy".

### Pitfalls like over-splitting and vague titles
- Fix: merge notes that only make sense together, or add the missing context.
- Fix: write titles as answers to likely searches: "Why X matters", "How to do Y", "X is a tradeoff between A and B".
- Fix: limit to 1-3 high-value links and always add a relationship sentence.

### Core premise: writing as thinking
- If you want better ideas, you need better intermediate artifacts.
- Permanent notes are the durable intermediate artifact.
- The book argues that you do not "think first, then write".

### Note types (fleeting, literature, permanent)
- Fleeting notes: temporary capture, processed quickly.
- Literature notes: source-bound notes that preserve context and citations.
- Permanent notes: standalone, atomic, written in your own words.

### Linking strategy and idea development
- example-of: A is an example of B (grounding)
- support: A supports B (evidence, mechanism, rationale)
- contrast: A differs from B (tradeoff, boundary, alternative)

### Workflow from reading to writing output
- You read about checklists in operations.
- You create a permanent note: "Do-confirm checklists prevent omission in complex work".
- You link it to notes about onboarding and reviews.

### Critiques and practical adaptations
- Pitfall: turning the system into a rigid ritual.
- Pitfall: obsessing over IDs or perfect structure.
- Pitfall: writing permanent notes that are really just quotes.

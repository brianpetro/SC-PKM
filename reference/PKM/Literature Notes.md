> [!summary]
> Source-bound notes that preserve context and quotes, ready for distillation and linking.

> [!When to use]
> If highlights keep turning into orphan quotes, this note shows how to write literature notes that stay traceable and ready for synthesis, even when you read quickly.

### What belongs in a literature note
- Source metadata: title, author, date, URL/ISBN, and where you found it.
- A short summary in your own words (3-7 bullets).
- Key claims (what the author is arguing).

### How to capture quotes with context and page refs
- Capture short quotes, not long passages.
- Always include page or location if available.
- Add the surrounding context: what question this quote answers.

### Distill into highlights and paraphrases
- Highlight the best lines (not everything).
- Add bold or "key takeaway" markers for the lines you want to reuse.
- Write a short paraphrase in your own words.

### Link to emerging concepts and permanent notes
- Link to concepts you already have (ex: [[Capturing Information]]).
- Create "candidate concepts" as headings inside the literature note.
- When a concept repeats across sources, promote it into a [[Permanent Notes]] note.

### Avoid copy-paste dumps and orphaned quotes
- Failure mode: "I pasted an entire article and never read it again."
- Failure mode: "Quotes with no provenance."
- Failure mode: "No path from sources to outputs."

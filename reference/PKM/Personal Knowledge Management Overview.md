### Plain definition and scope of PKM
- PKM is how you manage knowledge for reuse: capture -> connect -> retrieve -> apply.
- Personal: your learning, decisions, and work context.
- Practical: meant to improve outcomes, not just create an archive.

### Why PKM exists (problems it solves)
- Human memory is lossy and biased.
- Work and learning produce too many inputs to hold mentally.
- Context gets lost across time and teams.

### PKM mindset and guiding principles
- Capture with context (why you saved it, where it came from, what you might use it for).
- Prefer small, linkable notes over giant documents when reuse matters.
- Optimize for retrieval and reuse, not perfect organization.

### Core practices included in PKM
- Capturing with context (what + why + source).
- Processing and distilling into durable notes.
- Linking related ideas.

### Canonical list of PKM concepts with definitions
- Getting ideas and inputs into a trusted place fast.
- A staging area for raw capture before it becomes durable knowledge.
- Turning raw capture into a note type, action, or archive.

### How concepts relate as a system
- A simple system map: 1) Capture inputs into an inbox.
- 2) Process into note types (fleeting, literature, permanent).
- 3) Connect notes with links and minimal tags.

### Common misconceptions and corrections
- Misconception: "PKM is a second brain that stores everything." Correction: A PKM system should be selective.
- If you store everything, you stop trusting it.
- A small, high-signal vault beats a huge archive you avoid.
- Misconception: "PKM is storing everything." Correction: PKM is selective.
- Use filters and prune regularly.
- Misconception: "Tags are the system." Correction: Tags are for grouping.

### Minimal starter system and habits
- [[MOC Student]] for coursework capture, retention, and exam prep.
- One inbox for raw captures (notes, links, meeting scraps).
- A daily 5-15 minute processing habit.

### Examples across personal and professional life
- You learn a concept, write a note, and retrieve it later to make a better decision.
- You capture meeting decisions, link them to projects, and reuse them during planning and onboarding.
- You connect notes across topics and synthesize them into a draft.

### How PKM evolves with you over time
- Early stage: you mostly capture and label.
- Middle stage: you start linking, writing in your own words, and reusing notes.
- Later stage: you build stable "hubs" (indexes, dashboards, saved searches) and outputs become routine.

### Practical next steps for adopting PKM
- Create one note that states your current goals for PKM (work, learning, writing, projects).
- Then capture 5 items into your inbox with a one-line "intent" for each.
- You can find the right note in under 30 seconds.

### Pitfalls and fixes
- Pitfall: "Over-collecting inputs."
- Pitfall: "No processing habit."
- Pitfall: "Everything is tagged, nothing is findable."

### Practical behaviors that implement each concept
- Capture with a "why" line.
- Process 1-3 inbox items.
- Add 1-2 meaningful links.

## Key Practices
PKM refers to the systematic approach to capturing, organizing, and sharing personal knowledge. It involves a set of practices designed to help individuals manage the knowledge they acquire from various sources, enabling efficient retrieval and application in both personal and professional contexts.
- **Capture:** Quickly save ideas, quotes, links
- **Organize:** Use atomic notes, tags, and folders
- **Retrieve:** Smart filters, linked notes, semantic search
- **Share:** Turn notes into blogs, presentations, or teaching materials
